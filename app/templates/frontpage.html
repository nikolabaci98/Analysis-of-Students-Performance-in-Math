Are we the only beings in this enourmous universe? Are there other civilized societies? Could there be another Earth-like planet that fosters living creatures? For centuries these questions have pazzeled the minds of the most brilliant scientist and researchers. Finally, a decade ago, NASA lunched the Kepler telescop on a mission to search for exoplanets in star-systems besides our own, and possibly find habitable planets.





About the project

NASA has collected information of over 10,000 space objects since 2009 when the Kepler telescop was launched. These objects, also known as Kepler Object of Interest (KOI), are studied and investigated by performing a number of tests to determine if they are planets or not. As of May 7th, 2021 there are over 4383 confirmed exoplanets.

On this project, we are using a dataset taken from Kaggel that was last updated on 2017. KOI's in this dataset are labled as CONFIRMED (passed all the tests, it's a planet), FALSE POSITIVES (did not pass all the tests, it's not a planet), or CANDIDATES (under investigation). Our goal is to train a machine learning model on first two KOIs and use this model to predict the outcome of CANDIDATE KOIs.



Machine Learning


We used Decision Trees to train out model and make prediction. To find the best performing decision tree, we applied an algorithm (GridSearchCV) that combined different values for the parameters, and returned the decision tree with optimal parameters.

Our dataset is imbalanced with twice as many False Positives as Confirmed cases. Hence, before we feed the data to the Decision Tree, we used a method called SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique). With SOMTE we reached an equilibrated dataset with a 1:1 ratio.

For more information check out the jupyter notebook.
