Are we the only Earth in this Universe?

For centuries, this question has puzzeled the mind of many humans who wonder if we are the only beings in this vast and dark Universe. Could there be civilized societies, or maybe another Earth-like planet that fosters living creatures. In 1969, the American astronaut Nail Armstrong stated "That's one mall step for man, one gian leap for mankind", when we famously step foot on the Moon. Since then the space industry has evolved tremendously, especially with the rise of private companies and collaborations with government funded agencies like NASA.

Our project is based on Kepler exoplanet search results. NASA built a satellite known as the Kepler Space Observatory and launched it in 2009. The telescope is dedicated to searching for exoplanets in star systems besides our own, and possibly finding other habitable planets. As of May 7th 2021 there are over 4383 confirmed exoplanets in total. The telescope is still active and continues to collect new data on its extended mission. Our dataset, that we found on kaggle, is a cumulative record of all observed Kepler "objects of interest" (referred to throughout the project as koi or kepoi)- all of the approximately 10,000 exoplanet candidates Kepler has taken observations on.


Machine Learning

We created two Decision Tree models for this project. We used the first tree to determine how accurate the model is when trained with our data. Scores obtained by the first model were in the high 90th percentile, so we then decided to train the model in the whole dataset. Before we feed the data to the Decision Tree algorithm, we used a method called SMOTE that stands for Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique. This technique is used to handle unbalanced data between two classes, in our case, we had twice as many False Positive data than Confirmed cases. With SOMTE we reached an equilibrated dataset with a 1:1 ratio.

When constructing our decision trees, we utilized the Grid Search method. This algorithm scanned the dataset to customize optimal parameters for this model. Grid Search builds a model on each parameter combination possible and iterates through every parameter combination and before storing a model for each combination. Before implementing the Grid Search algorithm we had an accuracy score of 83.7% compared to 98.9% post implementation.

Results

Following the training of our best model, we make the prediction on NASA's 2114 unlabeled data or the CANDIDATE KOIs. Then we compare our predictions with the latest NASA database. It results that more than 100 KOIs are labeled correctly by our model, we have some wrong labels and the rest is still the future’s mystery. We are excited to see how our predictions align with the final results of the Kepler’s observation and calculation.
